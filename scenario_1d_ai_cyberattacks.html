Background — 2 weeks earlier

You have a social media account where you post short videos of yourself. You received a friend request from someone with a name similar to a classmate. Assuming it was them, you accepted. That person was actually an adversary — a scammer — pretending to be someone you knew.


Once connected, the adversary scraped audio samples of your voice from your videos. They fed those clips into an AI voice cloning tool. They then searched your social media to identify your relatives and called one of them, using your cloned voice to tell a fake emergency story — getting money wired to them.

Break It Down
Click each step to reveal how the attack worked. Annotate your thinking as you go.
Step 1 The fake friend request
↓ Click to reveal
The adversary created a fake account with a name very similar to someone you knew — a classmate. They sent you a friend request on social media. Because the name looked familiar, you accepted without verifying.

Key vulnerability: You accepted a connection from someone you had not verified in real life.
Step 2 Voice harvesting from social media
↓ Click to reveal
Once connected, the adversary could watch all your videos. They recorded or "scraped" clips of your voice from your posts. Modern AI voice cloning tools need only 30–60 seconds of clear audio to produce a realistic voice clone. A few minutes gives even better results.

Key vulnerability: Your public or semi-public videos contained extensive audio of your voice.
Step 3 AI voice cloning
↓ Click to reveal
The adversary used a freely available or low-cost AI voice cloning tool. These tools analyze the tone, pitch, rhythm, and speech patterns from audio samples. The resulting clone can speak any text in your voice — even things you've never said.

Key vulnerability: AI tools have made voice cloning cheap, fast, and accessible to anyone.
Step 4 Identifying the target
↓ Click to reveal
The adversary searched your social media profiles to identify people close to you — family members, older relatives — and found contact information for one of them. Social media often reveals relationships through tagged photos, comments, and profile links.

Key vulnerability: Your social media made your family relationships visible to strangers.
Step 5 The scam call
↓ Click to reveal
Using your cloned voice, the adversary called your relative. They delivered a fake emergency story designed to create panic: arrest, jail, bail money needed immediately. Urgency and fear are classic social engineering tactics — they prevent the victim from thinking clearly or verifying the story.

Key vulnerability: Your relative had no way to verify the call and acted under pressure.
Step 6 Money wired — scam complete
↓ Click to reveal
Your relative wired the money as instructed. Wire transfers are nearly impossible to reverse once sent. The adversary disappeared with the funds. The entire attack — from voice cloning to money in hand — can happen within hours.

Key vulnerability: Wire transfers are irreversible. There is no "undo."

Attack Type Identified
Vishing — Voice Phishing

Vishing (voice phishing) is a scam where an adversary calls someone pretending to be a trusted person to trick them into giving up money or information. It's like email phishing — but over the phone.

What makes this attack different from classic vishing is the use of AI voice cloning. Instead of just impersonating someone, the adversary replicated an actual person's voice using audio scraped from social media — making it nearly impossible for the victim to tell the difference.

ℹ This scenario is preventable. Each step in the attack chain had a point where the right action could have stopped it entirely.
🔍

Verify before accepting

Before accepting any friend request, message the person through a channel you already trust to confirm it's really them.

🔒

Restrict your audience

Set your social media videos to friends-only. The less public audio of you that exists, the harder it is to clone your voice.

🔑

Create a family code word

Agree on a secret word with close family. In any emergency call, they ask for the code. No code = it's a scam.

📞

Hang up and call back

If a family member gets a call from "you" asking for money, they should hang up and call your known number directly.

💬

Talk to your relatives

Tell older relatives about voice cloning scams. They need to know that even if a voice sounds familiar, it may not be real.

🚨

Report it immediately

If it happens, report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and contact your bank right away. Speed matters for wire transfers.
